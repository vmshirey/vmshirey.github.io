Vaughn M. Shirey

Perspective on Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion

We all come to science with different backgrounds and identities; unfortunately, science has continued to exclude certain individuals from participating as their whole selves, especially women, BIPOC, and LGBTQ+ people. The structures of sexism, racism, cis-heteronormativity, and economic disadvantage are pervasive in society and the acknowledgement and dismantling of these systems starts with promoting equitable access and support in education and career development. Few professions have the opportunity to directly target such social change, as both researchers and educators, faculty are well positioned to do so.

I have had the opportunity to engage with social justice and organizing activities mostly through my work as a labor organizer and the grievance officer for my graduate workers’ union, the Georgetown Alliance of Graduate Employees (GAGE). Through organizing with GAGE and by serving as a member of the bargaining committee, I had the opportunity to negotiate salaries for graduate workers across the campus which resulted in raising wages by as much as 44.4%. Economic hardship is one of the most important barriers to diversity, equity, and inclusion work and intersects strongly with race, gender-identity, and sexual orientation. Further, in my role as the grievance officer for GAGE, I am responsible for mediating conflict between graduate workers, supervisors, and university administration. In this position, I have also made it my calling to serve as counsel and advocate for student/worker issues beyond the scope of our contract. This has advocacy resulted in the retention of student visa status through mentor conflict and the creation of an alternative measures procedure that prioritizes immediate harm-reduction from harassment and discrimination during ongoing and often lengthy Title IX investigations. Finally, in this role, I have co-facilitated a series of workshops for graduate workers aimed at providing emotional and communication strategies for navigating conflict and harm.

Outside of the academic domain, I am an active participant in the realm of prison abolition and community-building. Over the course of the COVID-19 pandemic and continuing today, I have leveraged Black + Pink (a non-profit organization focused on prison abolition and reintroduction for LGBTQ+ and HIV+ inmates) networks to connect with several incarcerated individuals as pen-pals. My conversations with people who have engaged in some of the most harmful acts known to society have shifted my perspective on how inequity can manifest in unfortunate situations and how the aforementioned systems of racism, sexism, and other forms of discrimination can intersect to amplify inequity in cycles of repeated exclusion and harm. Some of these conversations have been extremely difficult, but the parallels to my own lived experiences as someone who has been excluded along aspects of my identity drive me to be a source of community for these inmates.

My particular interest in conflict and harm-reduction stem from my experiences growing up as a queer, neurodivergent person in a hostile educational environment. My science teachers were my heroes and the only people I could seek true solace in during some of the darkest parts of my life. Being that person for someone else is the highest calling I could have in life, and I am committed to front-line advocacy for those in need. While increasing the diversity of course materials, inviting speakers from diverse backgrounds to seminars, and citing work from diverse authors are all excellent strategies they represent, to me, a minimum commitment to diversity, equity, and inclusion. Instead, my past and future work in this sphere focuses on improving the immediate and long-term material and ocial conditions of excluded groups in science. In future roles, I am inspired to have the institutional and material power to actively recruit and train women, BIPOC, and LGBTQ+ individuals in cutting-edge and highly in-demand data science techniques. Further, I am committed to paying recruits above a living-wage while supporting their professional development through workshop and speaking opportunities at the department and conference level. Throughout this commitment, I cannot emphasize enough the “power of place” and how important it will be to actually embed myself in these communities in order to have high efficacy in my ability to recruit from these demographics. I am particularly interested in strengthening the community-college to research pipeline to meet this goal.

Meeting diversity, equity, and inclusion outcomes further the quality of our science and professional communities, but more than that, they work towards bettering our sense of belonging and community on the whole. My past and future work in this sphere is motivated by my experiences, engagement, and by my training and commitment to direct action, harm-reduction, mediation, and community-building. In my view, the only way forward is together, and our sense of togetherness comes from strong networks of support and care.
