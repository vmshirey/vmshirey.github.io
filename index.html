How are cold-adapted insects responding to climate change? My research investigates this question, among others, using butterflies as a model organism. I primarily use unstructured data from museum specimens and archives to make inferences about long-term changes over large spatial extents. In particular, I am interested in reconstructing historical patterns within the many dimensions of communities, morphology, and color.

I am currently a biology PhD candidate in the Ries Lab of Butterfly Informatics at Georgetown University, located in Washington, DC. Prior to coming to Georgetown, I was a Fulbright Finland Fellow at the Finnish Museum of Natural History/University of Helsinki in the Laboratory for Integrative Biodiversity Research .

I obtained my BS in environmental science with a focus in evolution and biodiversity from Drexel University in 2017. During that time, I worked in the Gelhaus Lab at the entomology collections in The Academy of Natural Sciences and as a curatorial assistant in the botany collections. I also completed up a dual-minor in bioinformatics and sustainable design. These experiences influenced me to incorporate creative computational methods into the study of natural history specimens to answer pressing questions about life on Earth.

My PhD work has been awarded a National Science Foundation Graduate Research Fellowship as well as Global Biodiversity Information Facility Young Researchers Award, which is given to a single PhD student annually. Outside of science, I self-publish my own creative work and actively participate in the Black+Pink incarcerated pen pals program for queer and HIV+ prisoners. I am passionate about community-building work and currently serve as the grievance officer and mediator for the Georgetown Alliance of Graduate Employees .

Mentorship & Teaching

Formal Instruction

Fall 2021, Georgetown University Guest Laboratory Instructor. General Ecology. Co-created a community ecology laboratory focusing on leaf-litter dwelling arthropods. Students learned how to identify major groups of arthropods, collect and sort samples using a Berlese funnel trap, and calculate biodiversity metrics from sample data to compare sites to one another.
Winter 2021, Georgetown University Laboratory Assistant. Global Climate Change Ecology. Assisted with instruction and technical troubleshooting of computational analyses in class.
Winter 2020, Georgetown University Graduate Teaching Fellow. Global Climate Change Ecology. Redeveloped several lab exercises to teach fundamental data science skills in R to both majors and non-majors. Assisted with lecture and laboratory material.
Fall 2019, Georgetown University Graduate Teaching Fellow. General Ecology. Assisted with grading laboratory materials. Guest lectured on global climate change and its impacts on biological populations and communities.
