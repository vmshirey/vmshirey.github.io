Date: May 13th, 2023; 10:30 AM ET | Location: TBD/Zoom | Host: Dr. Leslie Ries

How do butterfly communities and populations respond to climate across their ranges? My doctoral research used recent climate change and cold-adapted butterflies as a laboratory for examining which species are threatened by changing environments and how they may be adapting in terms of their morphology. My work is broadly organized into four chapters:

Core Research Question(s): What is the status of publicly available butterfly biodiversity data across the continent and how do biases in these data intersect with forecasted climate change? What regions are experiencing notable data gaps?

Approach: Various statistical techniques.

Core Take-away(s): Regions projected to experience the most drastic climatic changes are also often regions where we know the least about butterfly biodiversity. Completeness in our knowledge of butterfly biodiversity is predicted by high-human population densities, indicating a strong spatial bias of knowledge towards urban areas.

This work was conducted in collaboration with Michael Belitz, Vijay Barve, and Robert Guralnick. It is currently published in Ecography.
