Research Themes

Global Change, Macroecology, and Invertebrate Conservation

The core of my research interests centers around examining how global change processes (chiefly, climate and land-use change) have shaped invertabrate communities and populations. Much of this work involves using statistics to create historical reconstructions of past ecologies. I largely use hierarchical/state-space models to accomplish this goal.

Applications of Machine Learning to Biodiversity Science

The massive digitization of biodiversity data available from natural history museums and community science platforms is a tremendous asset that can be leveraged for scientific discovery by using machine learning approaches. While I have worked with natural language processing in the past, my current focus is on using computer vision to extract intraspecific trait data from specimen photographs.

Advances in Modeling Frameworks and Approaches

Lastly, I am interested in developing new modeling approaches to (a) identify causal linkages in ecology/biodiversity and (b) better integrate and leverage data from a variety of sampling designs/biodiversity facets into unified models. For example, developing state-space models that incorporate phenology and morphometrics is of high interest to me.
